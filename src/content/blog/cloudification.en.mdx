## A question with only one answer

Same model, same file, a question with only one right answer: eighteen. Depending on the leeway I gave
the agent, it answered right, wrong, or once completely off the rails: eighteen, fourteen, or a hundred
and seventeen. Nothing changed but its freedom of action, and the bill: counted in tokens, those units
of text you pay the machine for every time it thinks.

This small experiment convinced me of something larger: agentic AI is replaying the cloud's growing
pains, and we settle the difference in tokens.

The question was about a piece of code: how many of its functions return a certain kind of result?
What matters is that it has an exact answer, eighteen, and that it can be counted, the way you'd count
"how many contracts in this folder are fixed-term?". The catch, here, is that four of those functions
are written in an unusual way. Whoever just searches for them by keyword misses exactly those four and
counts only fourteen: search a page for a word without understanding its grammar, and you miss the ones
phrased differently. I asked this same question four times, of the same small model, varying each time
what I allowed it to do, eight trials per variant, on a file frozen at a public version so that anyone
can rerun the measurement. Here is what each way costs, and what it's worth.

- **The agent that reads everything.** It goes through the code itself, page after page. Right every
  time, but slow and expensive: eighteen tool calls on average to reconstruct what an index, a book's
  table of contents prepared in advance, would give at a glance.
- **The tinkerer agent.** We hand it the generic toolkit, keyword search and the right to combine it
  all like a virtuoso, but not the dedicated tool that understands the grammar of code. It's the
  objection you hear everywhere, *surely good tinkering is enough?* The data say no, and not "a little
  worse": across eight trials, five land around fourteen, three collapse to five. Almost never the right
  count, and unpredictable from one run to the next. The generic toolkit is powerful, but it carries no
  guarantee of exactness where the dedicated tool does.
- **The tooled agent, let loose in the wild.** It calls the right tool, the one that gives the exact
  answer, then goes off the rails: it widens the search area on its own, rummages through other folders,
  reruns, recounts. Half the trials don't even return a usable count, and one of them claimed a hundred
  and seventeen functions. In reality, it had extended its search, of its own accord, to a whole folder
  of files instead of the single one it was asked about.
- **The tooled agent, kept on a leash.** It calls the right tool, once, on the right area, and stops
  there. Eighteen out of eighteen, on each of the eight trials, the four tricky functions included. No
  variation. And, incidentally, seven times cheaper than the agent that reads everything.

You might think this last one wins by default, since the tool it calls is also the one that serves as
the reference. But no: the right answer, eighteen, I established it separately, cross-checked by hand and
by two other routes. The tool doesn't decree the truth, it finds it reliably, where the tinkerer agent,
with access to the same code, misses it and caps out at thirteen. The dividing line isn't "the tool is
right because it's the tool", it's "a question with an exact answer should be handled by what computes
it, not by what guesses it".

I'd had a factor of a hundred in mind, the engineer's fantasy of the perfect tool against the agent that
struggles. The real gap is seven. For the same result made more reliable, seven times cheaper is already
a deal you sign without hesitating: it was my expectation that was overblown, not the gain that's small.
But cost was only half the story, and the other half surprised me more. The right tool, on its own, is
not enough. The fourth agent had exactly the same tool as the third. What changes between them is the
frame: we fixed the fourth one's scope, capped the number of steps, forbade it from re-checking its own
output. I haven't teased apart which of these three restraints carries most of the effect, so I'll
refrain from making it a law. But together, they turn an erratic agent into an exact one. The third one
was free. It redefined its own task, and got lost.

## A category error

Beneath this result is a simple mistake. Counting those functions is not a matter of judgment: one
answer, verifiable, always the same. It's what I'd call a *mechanical* task, in the sense a calculator
is mechanical: same inputs, same output, every time. Handing it to a machine that *guesses*, however
gifted, and that can vary from one run to the next, is a category error, like flipping a coin to decide
whether 17 is a prime number. And the trap is insidious, because the probabilistic machine knows how to
imitate the deterministic one: it often gives the right answer. But "often" is not "always", and above
all, the moment you let it loose, it doesn't just answer: it reinterprets the question. That's what the
third agent did. Nobody asked it to comb through the whole repository; it decided on its own that this
was the right reading of the task.

## The same move as the cloud

This temptation has an exact economic precedent: the cloud. Before it, you bought servers, a capital
cost paid once. The cloud turned that capital into an expense: you rent compute by the hour, you own
nothing anymore, you pay as you go. Often a decisive step forward. But it had its trap, and it has a
name: *lift-and-shift*. Drop your old application onto rented machines as-is, without rethinking it,
then discover the bill: paying by the hour, indefinitely, for what you could have frozen once.

Agentic AI replays that film, one notch higher. We no longer rent compute, we rent reasoning, billed by
the word the machine produces (the famous "token"). The cost of writing a program, paid once, turns into
a tab you settle every time you rerun the agent. Having it reread an entire module to answer a question
an index would settle at a glance is lift-and-shift transposed: you pay endlessly, in tokens, for what
you could have frozen once and for all.

## The tooling debt, paid in tokens

Why do we do it anyway? The reason is real, not laziness to scold. Writing a reliable tool has an entry
cost. The quick-and-dirty move is free, but the robust tool, the one that doesn't trip on any awkward
case and that you can rerun a thousand times without surprise, costs effort to write. The agent abolishes
that entry cost: it does the work right now, without your having to build the tool. Put differently, what
you won't, or can't, pay for in development, **you pay for in tokens.**

People will object that agents already write little programs for these tasks, ones that count, that
search, that rename. And that's exactly the right answer: writing a program means freezing the answer
instead of re-guessing it. The problem was never the agent that codes, it's the agent that redoes the
same task by eye, without ever freezing or testing its tool. To code is to land on the right side of the
line. The cost of writing the tool is paid only once and amortizes from the first uses, the way you write
a recipe once to make it a hundred times; and more often than not the tool already exists, the agent just
has to use it.

Sometimes going freehand is rational: a one-off, throwaway task doesn't deserve its own tool. The trap is
when the throwaway becomes recurring. You then re-pay the token on every run of a task that should have
been frozen once, and you pay twice: in money, and in reliability, since the free agent doesn't return the
same answer twice.

## The dividing line

The analogy offers a simple decision test. Faced with a task: does it have a verifiable, stable answer? If
yes (list, count, rename, check a format, trace dependencies), it's deterministic: the agent shouldn't redo
it by eye, it should build the tool, then call it. If the answer instead calls for judging an open
situation, writing, exploring the unknown, then fallible judgment is in its place.

My measurement adds a condition I hadn't seen coming: calling the right tool isn't enough, you still have
to bound the agent around it. The regime that went off the rails had the right analyzer; we'd simply left
it free to reinterpret its task. Mind you, this doesn't mean trusting the tool blindly. Trust is earned,
and it's earned through tests: that's the whole point of having written it once and for good. My count of
eighteen, I didn't take it on faith either, I cross-checked it three ways before making it a reference. A
deterministic tool that's wrong would give an answer that's wrong and reproducible: the worst of both
worlds, since no one questions it anymore. So the rule isn't "don't verify", it's: **verify a deterministic
result with something deterministic.** A tested tool, a second pass, a schema, an invariant. Never a model
left to recount by intuition, because the probabilistic one turned loose on an exact output doesn't
validate it: it reopens the door you'd just closed.

The discipline that follows is concrete, and it's the one I apply: don't read the code from memory but call
an analyzer; don't rewrite a symbol by hand but call a rewriting tool that doesn't get it wrong; don't let
the agent judge on its own whether its work holds, but run it through a mechanical quality gate. Each time,
the move is the same: take away from the model the decision that has a right answer, and give it back only
where there isn't one.

## An illustration, not a law

Let's be honest about scope. One measurement, a single small model, a single task, eight trials per
regime: it's an illustration, not a proof. A more powerful model would probably go off the rails less, and
the cost gap depends on the task. I draw no science from it, I draw a direction, and it squares with what
engineering common sense already knows.

Because agentic AI is not a trap. It's a real economic shift, and often progress. But it's replaying the
cloud's growing pains: mistaking "the machine *can* do it" for "the machine *should* do it". The cloud
eventually learned to sort what gains from elasticity and what is better left frozen. Agentic AI isn't
there yet. The whole discipline lies in that sorting: recognizing what must stay deterministic, bounding it
firmly, and paying once in code for what you'd refuse to re-pay endlessly in tokens.

## Going further

*If the gist is enough for you, you can stop here. What follows is for whoever wants the sources and the
detail of the measurement.*

The idea that a model gains from delegating to tools what it does poorly is not new. Toolformer sums it up
in one line: language models *"struggle with basic functionality, such as arithmetic or factual lookup,
where much simpler and smaller specialized models excel"*.

- Schick et al., [*Toolformer: Language Models Can Teach Themselves to Use Tools*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.04761), NeurIPS 2023 — models learn to call a calculator, a search engine, rather than to imitate them.
- Anthropic, [*Building Effective Agents*](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents) and [*Tool use with Claude*](https://docs.anthropic.com/en/docs/build-with-claude/tool-use) — when to promote an action into a dedicated tool rather than leave it to a generic command.
- M. Casado & S. Wang, [*The Cost of Cloud, a Trillion Dollar Paradox*](https://a16z.com/the-cost-of-cloud-a-trillion-dollar-paradox/), a16z — the economic flip side of all-cloud, and a conclusion that resonates here: the largest margin hides "between cloud hardware and the unoptimized code running on it".

A few reproducibility notes, for whoever wants to rerun the calculation rather than take my word for it.
The model was Claude Haiku, deliberately a small model, where the effect is most visible; a more powerful
model would probably go off the rails less. The dedicated tool of the fourth agent is `ast_search`, the
code analyzer from [axm-forge](https://github.com/axm-protocols/axm-forge), the toolkit I develop; the
generic shell of the other agents is `grep` and the like. The measured module is frozen at a public
version. The detailed protocol, the thirty-two raw runs and the code are on
[the measurement page](/en/blog/cloudification-mesure).
